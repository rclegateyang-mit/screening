
** LEHD microdata questions **
* Data available for workers * 
Earnings (EHF/JHF): Quarterly earnings from state UI wage records. Near-universal coverage (>95% of employment; excludes independent contractors, federal employees, railroad workers). About 1.5% of job-quarters have imputed earnings due to underreporting.

Residence (ICF_US_RESIDENCE): Annual residential location at census-block level (lat/lon and FIPS geocode). 

Sex, date of birth, place of birth (ICF, Stage A): ~5% missing. Imputed via nonparametric KDE using Numident, Census, and ACS as sources.

Race and ethnicity (ICF, Stage B): ~20% missing. Imputed via CART + KDE conditioning on sex, DOB, earnings quartiles, POB, and coworker/co-resident demographics. Moderate quality; the missingness rate is nontrivial but conditioning variables are informative.

Education (ICF, Stage C): ~80-85% missing; only available for workers age 25+. Four categories (< HS, HS, some college, BA+). Observed values come from Census 2000 long form (~7% coverage) and ACS (~15% cumulative). Imputation uses a log-linear model + KDE with two quality tiers:
  - Full-information imputation (workers with pre-2009 earnings): conditions on sex, age, race, POB, earnings deciles, industry, coworker/co-resident demographics. Run once in 2010 and never updated.
  - Limited-information imputation (workers entering 2009+): conditions only on sex, age, race, ethnicity, POB. Does not use earnings or workplace characteristics, and draws from frozen 2010 PPDs.
  Out-of-sample accuracy is modest: 26-47% correct category assignment depending on education level (vs. 80-94% for non-imputed). 

Employer linkage: Workers are linked to establishments (SEIN/SEINUNIT) via UI records. For multi-unit employers, worker-to-establishment assignment is imputed (U2W) using establishment size and worker residence-to-establishment distance.

* Priority questions * 
1. Proxy for worker skill
Require (u_ij)_j \perp v_i | (w_j)_j, (xi_j)_j and \ln q_i strictly increasing in v_i 
Past wage:
- Transparent, availible, and for experienced workers can use previous-firm wage
- Concerns:
a. Contamination with location: my past wage reflects my location and my skill. If v_i is a function of past (d_ij)_j, it's likely that unobserved skill e_i is correlated with current (d_ij)_j; this is an exclusion violation for distance. Concretely, a high-skill worker who lived near low-wage firms may have low past wages to avoid long commutes; this worker's skill residual is large and positive, and correlated with their location.
b. Contamination with preference: my past wage reflects my preference and my skill. If v_i is a function of past (u_ij)_j, it's likely that unobserved skill e_i is correlated with current (u_ij)_j; this is an exclusion vilation for v_i. Concretely, a high-skill worker who idiosyncratically prefers a low-wage firm may have low past wages; this worker's skill residual is large and positive, and correlted with their preferences.
- Concern a. can be somewhat addressed by looking at past wages of my coworkers (my neighbors have past d_ij similar to mine), assuming that workers don't systematically sort near firms where they work
- Concern b. can be somewhat addressed by looking at past wages of my neighbors (my coworkers have past varepsilon_ij similar to mine), assuming that there is no spaital correlation in u_ij (which amounts to assuming that workers don't sort near firms they idiosyncratically prefer)
Measures based on axuillary demographic orincome data of my coworkers or neigbhors (e.g. census tract income measures) share similar properties
